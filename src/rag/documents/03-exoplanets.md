# Exoplanet Discovery and Characterization

## What Are Exoplanets?
Exoplanets are planets that orbit stars other than our Sun. The first confirmed detection of an exoplanet orbiting a Sun-like star was 51 Pegasi b in 1995, discovered by Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz, who received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2019 for this achievement. As of 2025, over 5,700 exoplanets have been confirmed in more than 4,200 planetary systems, with thousands more candidates awaiting confirmation.

## Detection Methods

### Transit Method
The transit method detects exoplanets by measuring the tiny dip in a star's brightness when a planet passes in front of it (transits). The amount of dimming reveals the planet's radius relative to its star — typically a fraction of a percent for Earth-sized planets and up to about 1-2% for Jupiter-sized planets. NASA's Kepler space telescope (2009-2018) used this method to discover over 2,600 confirmed exoplanets, revolutionizing the field. Its successor, TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite), launched in 2018, surveys nearly the entire sky for transiting planets around nearby bright stars. The transit method can also reveal atmospheric composition through transmission spectroscopy, where starlight filtered through the planet's atmosphere is analyzed.

### Radial Velocity (Doppler) Method
As a planet orbits a star, the planet's gravity causes the star to wobble slightly. This wobble shifts the star's spectral lines due to the Doppler effect — blueshifted when the star moves toward us, redshifted when it moves away. The radial velocity method measures these shifts to determine the planet's minimum mass and orbital period. This method is most sensitive to massive planets in close orbits. The first exoplanet around a Sun-like star, 51 Pegasi b, was found using this technique.

### Direct Imaging
Direct imaging captures actual photographs of exoplanets by blocking out the overwhelming light of the host star using a coronagraph or starshade. This is extremely challenging because stars are typically millions to billions of times brighter than their planets. Direct imaging works best for young, massive planets in wide orbits, which are still hot and luminous from formation. Notable directly imaged systems include HR 8799 (four giant planets) and Beta Pictoris b. The James Webb Space Telescope has pushed direct imaging capabilities to smaller, cooler planets.

### Gravitational Microlensing
When a star with a planet passes in front of a more distant star, the foreground star's gravity acts as a lens, magnifying the background star's light. If the foreground star has a planet, the planet creates an additional brief spike in the light curve. This method is sensitive to planets at larger orbital distances and lower masses but produces one-time events that cannot be repeated. NASA's Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope, expected to launch in the late 2020s, will use microlensing to survey for exoplanets.

### Astrometry
Astrometry measures the precise position of a star in the sky over time. A planet's gravitational pull causes the star to trace a tiny ellipse. The Gaia space telescope has been measuring stellar positions with unprecedented precision and is expected to reveal thousands of Jupiter-mass exoplanets through astrometric detection.

## Types of Exoplanets

### Hot Jupiters
Gas giants orbiting very close to their stars (orbital periods of a few days). These were among the first exoplanets discovered because they produce large transit signals and strong radial velocity signals. Example: 51 Pegasi b (orbital period 4.23 days, about half Jupiter's mass). Their existence was unexpected and challenged traditional models of planet formation, leading to theories of planetary migration.

### Super-Earths
Rocky planets with masses between 1 and 10 Earth masses. These have no analog in our Solar System. Some may be habitable if they orbit in their star's habitable zone. Example: LHS 1140 b, a rocky super-Earth in the habitable zone of a red dwarf star at 40 light-years distance.

### Mini-Neptunes
Planets with masses between about 2 and 10 Earth masses and thick gaseous envelopes. They occupy the size range between Earth and Neptune and are the most common type of planet found by Kepler. There appears to be a "radius gap" at about 1.5-2 Earth radii that separates rocky super-Earths from gaseous mini-Neptunes.

### Rogue Planets
Planets that do not orbit any star and instead drift through interstellar space. They may have been ejected from their parent systems through gravitational interactions. Microlensing surveys suggest there could be billions of rogue planets in the Milky Way.

## The Habitable Zone
The habitable zone (or "Goldilocks zone") is the region around a star where conditions could allow liquid water to exist on a planet's surface. The boundaries depend on stellar luminosity and the planet's atmosphere. For the Sun, the habitable zone extends roughly from 0.95 AU to 1.67 AU. For red dwarf stars, the habitable zone is much closer — often within 0.1-0.4 AU.

## Atmospheric Characterization
The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), launched in December 2021, has transformed exoplanet atmospheric science. Using transmission spectroscopy and direct emission measurements, JWST has detected water vapor, carbon dioxide, methane, and sulfur dioxide in exoplanet atmospheres. In 2023, JWST detected dimethyl sulfide in the atmosphere of K2-18b, a molecule that on Earth is only produced by life — though this detection remains debated.

## Notable Exoplanetary Systems

### TRAPPIST-1
A system of seven Earth-sized planets orbiting an ultracool red dwarf star 40 light-years away. Three of these planets (e, f, and g) are in the habitable zone. JWST has been studying this system extensively, finding that the innermost planets likely lack significant atmospheres.

### Proxima Centauri b
A roughly Earth-mass planet in the habitable zone of the nearest star to the Sun (4.24 light-years). Its habitability is uncertain due to Proxima Centauri's frequent stellar flares, which could strip away any atmosphere.

### Kepler-186f
The first Earth-sized planet found in the habitable zone of another star, orbiting a red dwarf 580 light-years away. Discovered in 2014, it receives about one-third the energy from its star that Earth receives from the Sun.

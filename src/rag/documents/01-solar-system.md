# The Solar System: A Complete Overview

## Formation and Age
The Solar System formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant interstellar molecular cloud. The vast majority of the system's mass is in the Sun, with the majority of the remaining mass contained in Jupiter. The four inner system planets — Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars — are terrestrial planets composed primarily of rock and metal. The four giant planets of the outer system are substantially more massive: Jupiter and Saturn are gas giants composed mainly of hydrogen and helium, while Uranus and Neptune are ice giants composed mostly of substances with relatively high melting points compared to hydrogen and helium, called volatiles, such as water, ammonia, and methane.

## The Sun
The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star (G2V) that contains 99.86% of the system's known mass. It has a surface temperature of about 5,778 K and a luminosity of 3.828 × 10²⁶ watts. The Sun's core temperature reaches approximately 15 million K, where hydrogen fusion produces the energy that powers the star. The Sun is currently about halfway through its main-sequence evolution, having fused about half of the hydrogen in its core. In approximately 5 billion years, the Sun will evolve into a red giant, expanding to engulf Mercury, Venus, and possibly Earth.

## Terrestrial Planets

### Mercury
Mercury is the smallest planet in the Solar System and the closest to the Sun. It has no atmosphere to retain heat, resulting in surface temperatures ranging from -180°C at night to 430°C during the day. Mercury has a large iron core that makes up about 85% of its radius, giving it the second-highest density of any planet (5.427 g/cm³). The MESSENGER mission (2011-2015) revealed water ice in permanently shadowed craters near the poles. Mercury's orbital period is 88 Earth days, and it rotates on its axis once every 59 Earth days.

### Venus
Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is similar in size and mass to Earth, earning it the nickname "Earth's twin." However, its thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide creates a runaway greenhouse effect with surface temperatures averaging 465°C — the hottest of any planet. Atmospheric pressure on Venus is 92 times that of Earth. Venus rotates retrograde (clockwise when viewed from above its north pole) and has the longest rotation period of any planet at 243 Earth days. The Magellan spacecraft mapped 98% of Venus's surface using radar in the early 1990s, revealing volcanic plains, highland regions, and over 1,600 major volcanoes.

### Earth
Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the largest of the terrestrial planets. It is the only known astronomical object to harbor life. Earth's atmosphere is 78% nitrogen and 21% oxygen. The planet has a strong magnetic field generated by its liquid iron outer core, which shields the surface from harmful solar radiation. Earth's Moon is the fifth-largest moon in the Solar System and was likely formed from debris after a Mars-sized body collided with Earth about 4.5 billion years ago (the Giant Impact Hypothesis). Earth's axial tilt of 23.44° is responsible for seasonal variations.

### Mars
Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and is known as the "Red Planet" due to iron oxide (rust) on its surface. Mars has the tallest volcano in the Solar System — Olympus Mons, standing 21.9 km above the surrounding terrain — and the deepest canyon, Valles Marineris, which stretches over 4,000 km. Mars has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos, which are likely captured asteroids. Evidence from rovers (Spirit, Opportunity, Curiosity, and Perseverance) strongly suggests Mars once had liquid water on its surface. The thin Martian atmosphere (95% CO₂) produces dust storms that can envelop the entire planet.

## Gas and Ice Giants

### Jupiter
Jupiter is the largest planet in the Solar System with a mass of 1.898 × 10²⁷ kg (318 Earth masses). Its Great Red Spot is a persistent anticyclonic storm larger than Earth that has been observed for over 350 years. Jupiter has at least 95 known moons, including the four Galilean moons: Io (the most volcanically active body in the Solar System), Europa (which likely has a subsurface ocean of liquid water), Ganymede (the largest moon in the Solar System), and Callisto. The Juno mission has been orbiting Jupiter since 2016, studying its atmosphere, magnetic field, and interior structure.

### Saturn
Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun, famous for its prominent ring system made primarily of ice particles and rocky debris. Saturn's rings extend from 6,630 km to 120,700 km above the equator. The planet has at least 146 known moons. Titan, Saturn's largest moon, is the only moon in the Solar System with a dense atmosphere and stable bodies of surface liquid (methane and ethane lakes). Enceladus, another moon, has geysers of water vapor erupting from its south pole, indicating a subsurface ocean. The Cassini-Huygens mission (2004-2017) provided extensive data on Saturn, its rings, and moons.

### Uranus
Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun and has the most extreme axial tilt of any planet at 97.77°, essentially rolling on its side as it orbits. This extreme tilt results in each pole getting around 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness. Uranus has 13 known rings and 28 known moons. Its blue-green color comes from methane in its atmosphere absorbing red light. Voyager 2 is the only spacecraft to have flown by Uranus, doing so in January 1986.

### Neptune
Neptune is the eighth and most distant planet in the Solar System. Despite receiving very little solar energy, Neptune has the strongest sustained winds of any planet, reaching speeds of 2,100 km/h. Its largest moon, Triton, orbits retrograde and is likely a captured Kuiper Belt object. Triton has active nitrogen geysers and a thin atmosphere. Neptune has 16 known moons and a faint ring system. Voyager 2 flew by Neptune in August 1989, the only spacecraft to have visited.

## Small Bodies

### Asteroid Belt
The asteroid belt lies between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter and contains millions of rocky bodies. The largest object in the belt is the dwarf planet Ceres (940 km diameter), which was visited by NASA's Dawn spacecraft in 2015. Most asteroids are irregularly shaped and composed of rock and metal.

### Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud
The Kuiper Belt is a region of icy bodies extending from Neptune's orbit (30 AU) to about 50 AU from the Sun. Pluto, reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006, is the most famous Kuiper Belt Object. NASA's New Horizons spacecraft flew by Pluto in 2015, revealing a complex world with mountains of water ice, nitrogen glaciers, and a thin atmosphere. The Oort Cloud, a theoretical spherical shell of icy objects, extends from about 2,000 AU to perhaps 100,000 AU, marking the boundary of the Sun's gravitational influence.

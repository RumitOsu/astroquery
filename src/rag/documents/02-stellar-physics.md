# Stellar Classification and Life Cycles

## What Are Stars?
Stars are massive, luminous spheres of plasma held together by gravity. They generate energy through nuclear fusion in their cores, primarily converting hydrogen into helium. The balance between the outward pressure from fusion energy and the inward pull of gravity determines a star's structure and behavior. Stars are the fundamental building blocks of galaxies and are responsible for producing most of the chemical elements heavier than hydrogen and helium through nucleosynthesis.

## Stellar Classification — The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram
Stars are classified by their spectral type based on surface temperature. The main spectral types, from hottest to coolest, are O, B, A, F, G, K, M (remembered by the mnemonic "Oh Be A Fine Guy/Girl, Kiss Me"). The Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram plots stellar luminosity against surface temperature, revealing distinct groupings:

- **O-type stars**: Surface temperature 30,000-50,000 K, blue, extremely luminous. Examples: Naos, Zeta Puppis. Rare — only about 0.00003% of main-sequence stars.
- **B-type stars**: 10,000-30,000 K, blue-white. Examples: Rigel, Spica.
- **A-type stars**: 7,500-10,000 K, white. Examples: Sirius, Vega.
- **F-type stars**: 6,000-7,500 K, yellow-white. Examples: Procyon, Canopus.
- **G-type stars**: 5,200-6,000 K, yellow. The Sun is a G2V star.
- **K-type stars**: 3,700-5,200 K, orange. Examples: Arcturus, Alpha Centauri B.
- **M-type stars**: 2,400-3,700 K, red. These are the most common stars, making up about 76% of all main-sequence stars. Examples: Proxima Centauri, Barnard's Star.

## Stellar Formation
Stars form in giant molecular clouds — vast regions of gas and dust in interstellar space with typical masses of 10,000 to 10 million solar masses. When a region of a molecular cloud becomes dense enough, it begins to collapse under its own gravity. As the cloud collapses, it fragments into smaller clumps, each of which may form a star.

The collapsing cloud heats up and forms a protostar surrounded by a circumstellar disk. When the core temperature reaches about 10 million K, hydrogen fusion ignites, and the protostar becomes a main-sequence star. The entire process from cloud collapse to main-sequence star takes roughly 10-15 million years for a Sun-like star, but can be as short as 100,000 years for massive O-type stars.

## Main-Sequence Evolution
During the main-sequence phase, a star fuses hydrogen into helium in its core. This is the longest phase of a star's life. The Sun has been on the main sequence for about 4.6 billion years and will remain there for another 5 billion years. More massive stars burn through their hydrogen much faster: a 10-solar-mass star will exhaust its fuel in only about 20 million years, while a red dwarf with 0.1 solar masses could remain on the main sequence for over a trillion years.

The mass-luminosity relation for main-sequence stars follows approximately L ∝ M^3.5, meaning a star twice as massive as the Sun is roughly 11 times more luminous.

## Post-Main-Sequence Evolution

### Low-Mass Stars (< 8 Solar Masses)
When a low-mass star exhausts the hydrogen in its core, the core contracts and heats up while the outer layers expand and cool, turning the star into a red giant. The star may undergo helium fusion in its core (the helium flash for stars under about 2.5 solar masses), producing carbon and oxygen. Eventually, the outer layers are expelled as a planetary nebula, leaving behind a white dwarf — a dense remnant about the size of Earth but with a mass comparable to the Sun. White dwarfs slowly cool over billions of years and eventually become black dwarfs, though the universe is not old enough for any black dwarfs to exist yet.

### High-Mass Stars (≥ 8 Solar Masses)
Massive stars undergo successive stages of nuclear fusion, burning helium, then carbon, neon, oxygen, and finally silicon, producing an iron core. Since iron fusion does not release energy (it consumes it), the core suddenly collapses when it exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 solar masses). The result is a core-collapse supernova — one of the most energetic events in the universe.

If the remaining core is between about 1.4 and 3 solar masses, it forms a neutron star — an incredibly dense object where protons and electrons are crushed together to form neutrons. A typical neutron star has a radius of only about 10 km but a mass of 1.4-2 solar masses. Some neutron stars are observed as pulsars, emitting beams of electromagnetic radiation from their magnetic poles.

If the core exceeds about 3 solar masses, not even neutron degeneracy pressure can halt the collapse, and a black hole forms.

## Stellar Remnants

### White Dwarfs
White dwarfs are supported against gravitational collapse by electron degeneracy pressure. They have typical masses of 0.5-0.8 solar masses and radii similar to Earth (~6,000 km). The maximum mass of a white dwarf is the Chandrasekhar limit, approximately 1.4 solar masses. If a white dwarf in a binary system accretes enough matter to exceed this limit, it undergoes a Type Ia supernova — these events are used as "standard candles" for measuring cosmic distances.

### Neutron Stars
Neutron stars are the densest objects that can be directly observed. A teaspoon of neutron star material would weigh about 6 billion tons on Earth. The fastest-spinning neutron star rotates 716 times per second. Magnetars are neutron stars with extremely powerful magnetic fields (10¹⁴ to 10¹⁵ gauss), which can produce intense bursts of X-rays and gamma rays.

## Notable Stars
- **Sirius**: The brightest star in the night sky, a binary system at 8.6 light-years distance
- **Betelgeuse**: A red supergiant in Orion, expected to go supernova within the next 100,000 years
- **Proxima Centauri**: The closest star to the Sun at 4.24 light-years, an M-type red dwarf with at least two confirmed exoplanets
- **Eta Carinae**: A massive, unstable star system with 100+ solar masses, a supernova candidate
- **UY Scuti**: One of the largest known stars with a radius about 1,700 times that of the Sun

# Black Holes and Neutron Stars

## What Are Black Holes?
A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing — not even light — can escape once it crosses the event horizon. Black holes are predicted by Einstein's general theory of relativity, which describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy. The boundary of a black hole is called the event horizon — not a physical surface, but a mathematical boundary beyond which the escape velocity exceeds the speed of light.

## Types of Black Holes

### Stellar-Mass Black Holes
Formed from the gravitational collapse of massive stars (typically those with initial masses above about 20-25 solar masses) after they exhaust their nuclear fuel. Stellar-mass black holes have masses ranging from about 3 to 100 solar masses. The first black hole candidate identified was Cygnus X-1, an X-ray binary system where a black hole accretes matter from a companion star. As of 2025, gravitational wave observatories have detected dozens of stellar-mass black hole mergers, with individual masses ranging from about 5 to 85 solar masses.

### Supermassive Black Holes
Found at the centers of most large galaxies, with masses ranging from millions to billions of solar masses. The Milky Way's central black hole, Sagittarius A* (Sgr A*), has a mass of about 4 million solar masses. The Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) captured the first direct image of a supermassive black hole's shadow in 2019 — M87*, at the center of galaxy M87, with a mass of 6.5 billion solar masses. In 2022, EHT released an image of Sgr A*. How supermassive black holes form and grow to such enormous sizes is an active area of research — they may grow from smaller "seed" black holes through accretion and mergers.

### Intermediate-Mass Black Holes
Black holes with masses between about 100 and 100,000 solar masses. These are the least well-understood category. Evidence for their existence comes from ultraluminous X-ray sources and gravitational wave detections. The gravitational wave event GW190521, detected in 2020, resulted from the merger of two black holes of about 85 and 66 solar masses, producing an intermediate-mass black hole of about 142 solar masses.

## Black Hole Properties

### The Event Horizon
The event horizon is the boundary beyond which nothing can escape. For a non-rotating (Schwarzschild) black hole, the radius of the event horizon is the Schwarzschild radius: Rs = 2GM/c², where G is the gravitational constant, M is the mass, and c is the speed of light. For a black hole with the mass of the Sun, the Schwarzschild radius is about 3 km. For Sgr A*, it is about 12 million km.

### Singularity
At the center of a black hole, general relativity predicts a singularity — a point of infinite density. Most physicists believe the singularity indicates that general relativity breaks down at this scale and that a theory of quantum gravity is needed to describe what actually happens at the center.

### Hawking Radiation
In 1974, Stephen Hawking theoretically predicted that black holes are not entirely black — they emit thermal radiation due to quantum effects near the event horizon. This Hawking radiation causes black holes to slowly lose mass and eventually evaporate. For stellar-mass black holes, this process is negligibly slow — a black hole with the mass of the Sun would take about 10⁶⁷ years to evaporate, far longer than the current age of the universe. Smaller black holes radiate more intensely and evaporate faster.

### No-Hair Theorem
The no-hair theorem states that a black hole can be completely described by just three properties: mass, electric charge, and angular momentum (spin). All other information about the matter that formed the black hole is lost behind the event horizon. This leads to the black hole information paradox — a conflict between general relativity (which says information is lost) and quantum mechanics (which says information must be preserved).

## Accretion and Jets
When matter falls toward a black hole, it typically forms a rotating accretion disk. Friction within the disk heats the material to millions of degrees, causing it to emit X-rays and other high-energy radiation. Some black holes produce powerful relativistic jets — beams of ionized matter ejected perpendicular to the accretion disk at nearly the speed of light. These jets can extend for thousands of light-years. When a jet points toward Earth, the object is called a blazar and appears extremely bright.

Active galactic nuclei (AGN) are supermassive black holes that are actively accreting material. Quasars are the most luminous type of AGN, visible across billions of light-years. The most luminous quasars can outshine their entire host galaxy by a factor of 100 or more.

## Gravitational Waves from Black Holes
The Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO) made the first direct detection of gravitational waves on September 14, 2015 (announced February 2016). The signal, GW150914, came from the merger of two black holes of about 36 and 29 solar masses, producing a final black hole of about 62 solar masses — with 3 solar masses worth of energy radiated as gravitational waves. This discovery confirmed a key prediction of Einstein's general relativity and earned the 2017 Nobel Prize in Physics. LIGO and its European counterpart Virgo have since detected dozens of black hole mergers.

## Neutron Stars

### Formation and Structure
Neutron stars form when a massive star (8-25 solar masses) explodes as a core-collapse supernova. The remaining core, with a mass of about 1.4-2.1 solar masses, collapses to a radius of only about 10-12 km. The density is staggering — a sugar-cube-sized piece of neutron star material would weigh about 1 billion tons on Earth. Neutron stars are composed primarily of neutrons packed together at nuclear density (about 4 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³).

### Pulsars
Pulsars are rapidly rotating neutron stars that emit beams of electromagnetic radiation from their magnetic poles. As the star rotates, these beams sweep across the sky like a lighthouse, producing regular pulses when pointed toward Earth. The first pulsar was discovered by Jocelyn Bell Burnell and Antony Hewish in 1967. Millisecond pulsars can spin at up to 716 rotations per second (PSR J1748-2446ad). Pulsars are extraordinarily precise clocks and are used to test general relativity, detect gravitational waves, and study the interstellar medium.

### Magnetars
Magnetars are neutron stars with the most powerful magnetic fields in the known universe — up to 10¹⁵ gauss (about a quadrillion times stronger than Earth's magnetic field). They can produce enormous bursts of X-rays and gamma rays. The most powerful magnetar burst ever recorded was the December 2004 event from SGR 1806-20, which was so energetic it temporarily compressed Earth's magnetosphere despite occurring 50,000 light-years away.

### Neutron Star Mergers
When two neutron stars spiral together and merge, they produce gravitational waves, a burst of gamma rays, and an explosion called a kilonova. The first neutron star merger detected by gravitational waves (GW170817, August 2017) was also observed across the electromagnetic spectrum — from gamma rays to radio waves — making it the first multi-messenger astronomical event. Neutron star mergers are responsible for producing many of the universe's heaviest elements, including gold, platinum, and uranium, through rapid neutron capture (r-process) nucleosynthesis.

# Cosmology and the Big Bang

## The Big Bang Theory
The Big Bang theory is the prevailing cosmological model describing the origin and evolution of the universe. It states that the universe began as an extremely hot, dense state approximately 13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding and cooling ever since. The theory is supported by three key pieces of observational evidence: the expansion of the universe (Hubble's law), the cosmic microwave background radiation, and the observed abundances of light elements (Big Bang nucleosynthesis).

## Timeline of the Universe

### Planck Epoch (0 to 10⁻⁴³ seconds)
The earliest period of the universe, when all four fundamental forces (gravity, electromagnetism, strong nuclear, and weak nuclear) were unified. Our current physics cannot describe this era because quantum gravitational effects dominate and we lack a complete theory of quantum gravity.

### Inflation (10⁻³⁶ to 10⁻³² seconds)
A period of exponential expansion proposed by Alan Guth in 1981. During inflation, the universe expanded by a factor of at least 10²⁶ in a fraction of a second. Inflation explains why the universe appears flat, homogeneous, and isotropic on large scales, and why we don't observe magnetic monopoles. It also predicts the slight density fluctuations that seeded the large-scale structure of the universe. These predictions have been confirmed by observations of the cosmic microwave background.

### Quark Epoch (10⁻¹² to 10⁻⁶ seconds)
All four fundamental forces had separated. The universe was a quark-gluon plasma too hot for quarks to bind into protons and neutrons.

### Hadron Epoch (10⁻⁶ to 1 second)
The universe cooled enough for quarks to combine into hadrons (protons and neutrons). By the end of this epoch, most hadrons and anti-hadrons had annihilated each other, leaving a small excess of matter over antimatter — about one particle per billion survived. This slight asymmetry, called baryogenesis, is why the universe contains matter at all.

### Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (10 seconds to 20 minutes)
The universe cooled to about 1 billion K, cool enough for protons and neutrons to fuse into light nuclei. This process produced approximately 75% hydrogen, 25% helium-4, with trace amounts of deuterium, helium-3, and lithium-7 by mass. These predicted abundances match observations, providing strong evidence for the Big Bang theory.

### Recombination and the Cosmic Microwave Background (380,000 years)
When the universe cooled to about 3,000 K, electrons combined with nuclei to form neutral atoms for the first time. This made the universe transparent to light — photons could travel freely for the first time. These photons, redshifted by the expansion of the universe, form the cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation observed today at a temperature of 2.725 K. The CMB was discovered accidentally by Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson in 1964, earning them the Nobel Prize.

### Dark Ages (380,000 to ~200 million years)
After recombination, the universe was filled with neutral hydrogen gas. No stars had yet formed, so the universe was dark. Gravity slowly pulled gas into denser regions, setting the stage for the first stars.

### Cosmic Dawn and Reionization (~200 million to 1 billion years)
The first stars (Population III stars) formed from primordial hydrogen and helium. These were likely very massive (100-1000 solar masses) and short-lived, producing the first heavy elements through fusion and supernovae. Their ultraviolet radiation began to reionize the neutral hydrogen. The first galaxies formed during this era. JWST has observed galaxies from this epoch, some existing just 300-400 million years after the Big Bang.

## Expansion of the Universe

### Hubble's Law
In 1929, Edwin Hubble discovered that galaxies are moving away from us at speeds proportional to their distance: v = H₀ × d, where H₀ is the Hubble constant. This was the first observational evidence for the expanding universe. The current best estimate of the Hubble constant is approximately 67-73 km/s/Mpc, though there is a significant tension between values measured by different methods (the "Hubble tension").

### Dark Energy and Accelerating Expansion
In 1998, two independent teams studying Type Ia supernovae discovered that the expansion of the universe is accelerating, not decelerating as expected. This discovery, which earned the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics, implies the existence of a mysterious repulsive force called dark energy. Dark energy constitutes about 68% of the total energy density of the universe. Its nature is one of the biggest unsolved problems in physics. The simplest explanation is Einstein's cosmological constant (Λ), representing a constant energy density of empty space.

## Dark Matter
Observations show that visible matter accounts for only about 5% of the universe's total mass-energy content. About 27% is dark matter — matter that does not emit, absorb, or reflect light but interacts through gravity. Evidence for dark matter includes:
- Galaxy rotation curves: Stars in outer galaxy regions orbit faster than expected from visible mass alone
- Gravitational lensing: Galaxy clusters bend light more than their visible mass would predict
- CMB anisotropies: The pattern of temperature fluctuations in the CMB requires dark matter to match observations
- Large-scale structure: Computer simulations of galaxy formation require dark matter to produce structures matching observations

Leading dark matter candidates include Weakly Interacting Massive Particles (WIMPs) and axions. Despite decades of searching, dark matter particles have not been directly detected in laboratories.

## The Cosmic Web
On the largest scales, matter in the universe is organized into a cosmic web of filaments, walls, and voids. Galaxy clusters sit at the intersections of filaments, while vast voids (some hundreds of millions of light-years across) contain very few galaxies. This structure grew from the tiny density fluctuations imprinted during inflation and amplified by gravity over cosmic time.

## The Fate of the Universe
The ultimate fate of the universe depends on its total energy density and the nature of dark energy:
- **Heat Death / Big Freeze**: If dark energy remains constant, the universe will expand forever, eventually becoming too diffuse and cold for any organized structures to exist. Stars will burn out, black holes will evaporate via Hawking radiation, and the universe will approach maximum entropy.
- **Big Rip**: If dark energy strengthens over time, the expansion could accelerate until it tears apart galaxies, stars, planets, and eventually atoms.
- **Big Crunch**: If dark energy weakens or reverses, gravity could eventually halt the expansion and cause the universe to collapse back on itself. Current observations disfavor this scenario.
